Causes, Diagnosis and Symptoms

Causes of PCOS

Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) develops when the ovaries produce excessive amounts of androgens — male hormones present in both men and women but usually in small amounts in females. This hormonal imbalance can interfere with the normal development and release of eggs during the menstrual cycle.

Key contributing factors:

  • Insulin resistance: When cells don’t respond properly to insulin, the body produces more of it, which can increase androgen production.
  • Hormonal imbalance: Higher levels of luteinizing hormone (LH) or lower levels of sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG) can disrupt ovulation.
  • Genetics: PCOS often runs in families, suggesting a hereditary link.
  • Low-grade inflammation: Women with PCOS may have low-grade inflammation that stimulates cyst formation.
  • Lifestyle and environmental factors: Unhealthy diet, lack of physical activity, and high stress may worsen symptoms.

Symptoms

Symptoms can vary greatly depending on the specific condition. Common signs to watch for include:

  • Changes in menstrual cycle patterns (irregular or missed periods)
  • Persistent pelvic pain or discomfort
  • Unexplained weight gain or loss
  • Skin changes such as acne or dark patches
  • Fatigue and mood fluctuations

Not all symptoms indicate a serious condition; however, if they persist or worsen, a medical consultation is advised.

Diagnosis

There is no single test for PCOS; doctors usually confirm it based on a combination of symptoms, blood tests, and imaging.

  • Medical history review: Discussing menstrual cycle patterns, weight changes, skin problems, and family history of PCOS or diabetes.
  • Physical examination: Checking for excess hair growth, acne, and other signs of high androgen levels.
  • Blood tests: Measuring hormone levels (LH, FSH, testosterone, insulin, thyroid function) to rule out other causes.
  • Pelvic ultrasound: Imaging to check if ovaries have multiple small cysts and to assess the thickness of the uterine lining.
  • Exclusion of other conditions: Such as thyroid disorders, hyperprolactinemia, or congenital adrenal hyperplasia.

Accurate diagnosis is essential for designing an effective treatment plan tailored to the patient’s needs.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

Q1: Difference between PCOS, PCOD, and PCO

PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome) is a complex hormonal and metabolic disorder in which the ovaries produce higher-than-normal levels of androgens (male hormones). This can disrupt ovulation, cause irregular menstrual cycles, and lead to symptoms such as acne, excessive hair growth, weight gain, and infertility. PCOS often involves systemic effects, including insulin resistance and an increased risk of metabolic conditions.

PCOD (Polycystic Ovary Disease) is a condition where the ovaries develop multiple immature or partially mature eggs, which can turn into small cysts. This is often due to hormonal imbalances and lifestyle factors. PCOD is generally considered less severe than PCOS, and many women with PCOD can regulate their cycles and conceive with lifestyle modifications.

PCO (Polycystic Ovaries) refers only to the ultrasound finding of ovaries containing multiple small cysts or follicles. PCO on its own is not necessarily a disorder — it can be a normal variation in some women. However, PCO may also be seen in women with PCOD or PCOS. The presence of PCO alone does not mean a woman has PCOS unless it is accompanied by other symptoms such as hormonal imbalances or irregular cycles.

In summary: PCO is an ovarian appearance, PCOD is an ovarian condition, and PCOS is a broader syndrome affecting the body’s hormonal and metabolic systems. All women with PCOS will have polycystic ovaries, but not all women with polycystic ovaries have PCOS.

Q2: Are all menstrual irregularities a sign of a serious problem?

Not necessarily. Menstrual changes can occur due to stress, dietary habits, or temporary hormonal fluctuations. However, prolonged irregularities should be discussed with a healthcare provider.

Q3: Can lifestyle changes help in managing symptoms?

Yes. Maintaining a balanced diet, regular exercise, and stress management can significantly improve symptoms and overall well-being.

Q4: How often should I undergo routine check-ups?

It is generally recommended to have a gynecological check-up at least once a year, or more frequently if symptoms persist or worsen.

Q5: What are the signs of PCOD?

PCOD symptoms include irregular menstrual cycles, weight gain, acne, excessive hair growth, mood swings, and sometimes difficulty conceiving. These symptoms are often milder than PCOS and can improve with lifestyle changes.

Q6: How is PCOD different from PCOS?

While PCOD primarily refers to an ovarian condition with cyst formation and some hormonal imbalance, PCOS is a wider syndrome involving systemic hormonal and metabolic effects. PCOS usually has more severe symptoms, including higher risks of insulin resistance, diabetes, and cardiovascular issues. PCOD can often be managed effectively with lifestyle modifications and is generally less severe than PCOS.

Q7: What are the signs of PCOS?

PCOS symptoms vary from person to person, but common signs include irregular or missed periods, excessive hair growth on the face or body (hirsutism), acne or oily skin, weight gain (especially around the abdomen), thinning scalp hair, and difficulty getting pregnant. Some women may also experience mood changes and darkened skin patches, particularly around the neck or underarms.

Q8: What parts of the body are affected by PCOS?

Although PCOS originates in the ovaries, it can affect the entire body. Hormonal imbalances can influence the skin, hair growth patterns, metabolism, weight regulation, and reproductive system. PCOS also has systemic effects, such as increasing the risk of type 2 diabetes, high cholesterol, and heart disease.

Q9: How is PCOS diagnosed (what are the tests for PCOS)?

There is no single test for PCOS. Diagnosis typically involves:

  • Medical history and symptom review: Menstrual patterns, weight changes, skin/hair issues
  • Physical examination: Signs of high androgen levels
  • Blood tests: Testosterone, LH, FSH, insulin, thyroid function
  • Pelvic ultrasound: To look for multiple small cysts on ovaries and uterine lining

Doctors often use the Rotterdam criteria, confirming PCOS if at least two of these are present: irregular ovulation, high androgen levels, or polycystic ovaries on ultrasound.

Q10: What causes PCOS?

The exact cause of PCOS is not fully understood, but it is believed to involve:

  • Insulin resistance: Leading to increased androgen production
  • Hormonal imbalances: Elevated LH and androgens
  • Genetic predisposition: Runs in families
  • Low-grade inflammation: Stimulates ovarian androgen production
Q11: Is PCOS inherited (run in families)?

Yes, PCOS can have a genetic component. If a mother or sister has PCOS, there is a higher chance of developing it. Lifestyle and environmental factors also influence symptoms.

Q12: What is the difference between having polycystic ovaries and having PCOS?

Polycystic ovaries (PCO) refer only to the ultrasound appearance of multiple cysts and may be normal.
PCOS is a syndrome involving symptoms and hormonal imbalances including irregular cycles and metabolic issues.

Q13: How common is PCOS and how common are polycystic ovaries?

PCOS affects about 1 in 10 women of reproductive age worldwide. Polycystic ovaries are found in up to 1 in 3 women, many without symptoms.

Q14: If I do not have polycystic ovaries, can I still have PCOS?

Yes. PCOS can be diagnosed without polycystic ovaries if other criteria like irregular ovulation and high androgens are met.
